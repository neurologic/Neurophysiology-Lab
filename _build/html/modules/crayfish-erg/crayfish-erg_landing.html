Week 6. ElectroRetinoGram¶

Receptor cells typically respond to stimuli with prolonged, graded potentials. Some receptor cells also have spike-generating mechanisms. Photoreceptor cells in the eyes of arthropods like the crayfish generally cannot produce spikes.

The electroretinogram (ERG) is a measurement of the summed (net) potential of many photoreceptors (recorded extracellularly). The waveform of the ERG is a good representation of intracellular events in the photoreceptors, as demonstrated by simultaneous intra- and extra-cellular measurement of photoreceptor potentials1. As in other extracellular recordings, depolarization of the cells appears as a negative potential to the electrode outside the cells.

Fig. 6 Simultaneous intracellular (left) and extracellular (right; plotted negative potential upwards) record of photoreceptor potentials in Astacus retina in response to a brief pulse of light (time base 1s total).1¶

Photoreceptors transform light energy into electrical energy via second messengers. Photoreceptor transduction has mostly been studied in Drosophila (another species in the Phylum Arthropoda)2. Transient receptor protein (TRP) channels are the ultimate target of the molecular cascade initiated by rhodopsin-detected photons. You will notice a lag between the time in which the second messenger system is activated by rhodopsin and the opening of TRP channels.

Fig. 7 Cartoon of the proteins involved in the transduction of light energy into electrical energy. The Gq α-subunit acts as a diffusible shuttle between activated rhodopsin and the complex.2¶

Changing the brightness of the light will change the number of rhodopsin molecules that are activated, and thus the amount of second messenger that is produced. This is analogous to varying the amount of transmitter at a metabotropic synapse.
